<table>
  <caption>Sensory Evaluation Indicators and Stepwise Methods for Fermented and Dry-Cured Meat Products</caption>
  <tr><th>Sensory attribute</th><th>Indicator (what to notice)</th><th>Step-by-step evaluation method</th><th>Common defect cues to record</th></tr>
  <tr><td>External appearance</td><td>Integrity of casing/surface; even shrinkage; absence of slime; expected surface dryness level</td><td>(1) Observe whole piece under natural or equivalent light. (2) Rotate 360° and check for wrinkles, tears, slime. (3) Lightly touch with glove to judge tackiness vs dry feel.</td><td>Slime, tearing, excessive wrinkles</td></tr>
  <tr><td>Cut-surface color</td><td>Lean color typical of fermented/dry-cured products; fat color (white/cream); color uniformity; absence of green/black spots</td><td>(1) Make a clean perpendicular cut. (2) Inspect within 30 s to avoid oxidation bias. (3) Compare lean and fat zones and note gradients or rings.</td><td>Discoloration ring, dark core, green specks</td></tr>
  <tr><td>Odor (fermentation note)</td><td>Expected tangy/fermented aroma; spice/smoke notes if applicable; absence of putrid, ammonia, or solvent-like odors</td><td>(1) Smell the intact surface first. (2) Smell immediately after cutting (closer to 2–3 cm). (3) Take 2 short sniffs + 1 longer sniff; record intensity and descriptors.</td><td>Putrid, ammonia, solvent, moldy</td></tr>
  <tr><td>Surface mold (when present)</td><td>Color and distribution of desirable mold; absence of colored/patchy undesirable molds; no fuzzy overgrowth where not expected</td><td>(1) Visually assess distribution and hue. (2) If needed, gently wipe a small area with dry sterile cloth to see if it smears. (3) Record whether mold is adherent and uniform.</td><td>Black/green patches, wet smear, uneven colonies</td></tr>
  <tr><td>Flavor balance</td><td>Salt level, acidity, spice, cured flavor; absence of harsh sourness, bitterness, or rancid fat flavor</td><td>(1) Take a 3–5 g slice at room temperature. (2) Chew 15–20 times; note initial taste then aftertaste at 10 s and 30 s. (3) Rinse mouth with water between samples.</td><td>Harsh sour, bitter, rancid, metallic</td></tr>
  <tr><td>Texture / sliceability</td><td>Firmness, cohesiveness, slice integrity; absence of crumbling, gumminess, or overly hard texture</td><td>(1) Attempt slicing at 2–3 mm thickness. (2) Note whether slices hold together. (3) Bite test: first-bite resistance then breakdown during 20 s chewing.</td><td>Crumbling, gummy mass, overly hard bite</td></tr>
  <tr><td>Juiciness / fat perception</td><td>Moistness vs dryness; fat melt and lubrication; waxy fat mouthfeel</td><td>(1) Evaluate at room temperature to allow fat release. (2) During first 10 chews, judge lubrication. (3) Note waxy sensation on palate after swallowing/spitting.</td><td>Dry mouthfeel, waxy fat, greasy coating</td></tr>
  <tr><td>Aftertaste cleanliness</td><td>Persistence of pleasant cured notes; absence of lingering off-flavors</td><td>(1) After swallowing/spitting, wait 60 s without eating. (2) Record aftertaste intensity and character at 30 s and 60 s.</td><td>Lingering rancid, musty, bitter</td></tr>
</table>
