    <tr><td>Feet-Together Jumps (in place)</td><td>Keep the torso tall with ribs stacked over the pelvis; brace the abdomen so the lumbar spine does not arch as the arms move.</td><td>Land toe-to-midfoot first and let the knees track over the toes; absorb quietly by bending at ankles, knees, and hips together rather than stiff-legged.</td><td>Drive the floor away with both legs equally; jump height should come from a quick triple-extension, not from throwing the shoulders upward.</td><td>Heels slamming first, knees collapsing inward, or excessive forward lean that makes the jump look like a hop-and-fall.</td></tr>
    <tr><td>Side-Close Jumps (left/right)</td><td>Stay centered with shoulders level; when closing the feet, keep the pelvis square rather than twisting to chase the stepping foot.</td><td>Control the lateral shift: step wide with a soft knee, then close quickly and land with the same cushioning on both sides to keep rhythm even.</td><td>Push laterally from the stance leg and use inner-thigh engagement to draw the legs together in the air; keep the jump elastic rather than high.</td><td>Overstriding so the body tilts, landing with straight knees, or letting the closing foot “drag” late and break timing through loss of control.</td></tr>
    <tr><td>Step-and-Turn (multi-step rotation)</td><td>Turn around the vertical axis with an upright spine; keep head and chest lifted so the rotation looks clean rather than folded.</td><td>Each step should have a cushioned contact; avoid slamming the final closing step—finish the turn with a stable base before the next jump.</td><td>Create rotation from the hips and controlled foot placement; keep step length consistent so the turn speed is produced by alignment, not rushing.</td><td>Over-rotating the shoulders first, wobbling on the last step, or uneven step sizes that cause drifting off the intended facing.</td></tr>
    <tr><td>Run-Jump Steps / Light Hops (alternating)</td><td>Keep the torso quiet and lifted; the swing leg folds under with a pointed toe while the stance leg stays springy, not rigid.</td><td>Use a “quiet landing” pattern: contact through the forefoot and let the heel lower only as needed; switch legs with continuous cushioning.</td><td>Initiate the hop from the ankle-knee elastic rebound; keep the amplitude moderate so speed stays crisp without losing posture.</td><td>Excessive bounce that throws the trunk, landing flat-footed, or pulling the knee forward instead of folding the lower leg back.</td></tr>
    <tr><td>Lunge / Forward Step with Hip Drive</td><td>Stack the torso over the pelvis with a proud chest; keep the front knee aligned with the second toe and avoid knee drift inward.</td><td>Place the front foot solidly before shifting weight; the back foot should assist balance rather than collapsing the posture.</td><td>Drive the hip forward/up with control; increase range through hip extension while keeping the abdomen braced to prevent low-back compression.</td><td>Front knee shooting past the toes due to a short stance, pelvis twisting, or leaning forward to “fake” depth.</td></tr>
    <tr><td>Half Squat Pulses / Open-Stance Pulses</td><td>Keep hips back slightly with the spine long; knees remain over toes and do not cave inward during repeated pulses.</td><td>Maintain continuous tension—avoid bouncing off locked joints; pulses should be absorbed by the legs, not by a collapsing chest.</td><td>Generate the pulse from thigh and glute engagement; keep a consistent depth and rhythm so the movement reads powerful, not shaky.</td><td>Depth changing every rep, heels lifting unintentionally, or bending from the waist so the back rounds and the pulse loses line.</td></tr>
    <tr><td>Flick-Kick Jump / Knee-Fold Action</td><td>Hold the trunk tall and stabilize the pelvis; the working knee stays controlled so the lower leg “flicks” without hip hiking.</td><td>Land the supporting foot with cushioning and immediate readiness for the opposite side; avoid a heavy landing that breaks elasticity.</td><td>Initiate from the thigh and keep the flick height moderate (roughly 30°–40°); toes stay pointed and the leg extends cleanly.</td><td>Kicking too high with a loose knee, throwing the shoulders back, or landing stiff-legged and losing the spring into the next action.</td></tr>
