    <tr><td>Speed control</td><td>Speed managed primarily by turn shape and pressure, not by emergency braking.</td><td>Speed remains controlled and appropriate; braking is integrated into turns rather than abrupt.</td><td>Sudden hockey stops, repeated panic skids, or runaway speed.</td></tr>
    <tr><td>Absorption & extension</td><td>Legs flex to absorb bump crests and extend into troughs.</td><td>Visible flexion at crests and extension in troughs; upper body stays relatively level.</td><td>Rigid legs causing bouncing; repeated loss of contact or being thrown off line.</td></tr>
    <tr><td>Ski-snow contact</td><td>Skis track continuously with minimal airborne time.</td><td>Skis stay on snow through most bumps; any unweighting is brief and controlled.</td><td>Frequent uncontrolled airtime; skis slap down causing balance breaks.</td></tr>
    <tr><td>Upper-body discipline</td><td>Torso faces generally downhill; hands forward and stable.</td><td>Minimal excessive rotation; hands remain visible and forward, supporting balance and timing.</td><td>Arms drop behind hips; torso rotates aggressively to force turns.</td></tr>
    <tr><td>Turn initiation</td><td>Turns initiated with edge/pressure release and steering, not with stemming.</td><td>Initiation is smooth; skis remain parallel without persistent wedge/stem entries.</td><td>Repeated stem starts; wedge used as primary control strategy in bumps.</td></tr>
    <tr><td>Recovery & stability</td><td>Ability to manage minor errors without stopping.</td><td>Corrects small balance errors and continues; no falls within the assessment segment.</td><td>Fall or near-fall requiring a stop/restart; inability to regain balance while moving.</td></tr>
