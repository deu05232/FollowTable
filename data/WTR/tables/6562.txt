# NewYork-Presbyterian/Queens - Skin Pigment Disorders

| Pigment disorder | Albinism | Melasma | Pigment loss after skin damage | Vitiligo |
| --- | --- | --- | --- | --- |
| Characteristics | This rare, inherited disorder is characterized by a total or partial lack of melanin in the skin, compared to the pigmentation of siblings and parents. Albinos (people with albinism) have white hair, pale skin, and pink eyes. | Dark brown, symmetric patches of pigment on the face. During pregnancy, this is called the mask of pregnancy. | Sometimes, following an ulcer, blister, burn, or infection, the skin does not replace some of the pigment in that area. | Smooth, white patches in the skin, vitiligo is caused by the loss of pigment-producing cells in the skin (melanocytes). The white patches are very sensitive to the sun. |
| Treatment | There is no cure for albinism. Albinos should avoid sunlight because they lack melanin, a natural protection from sunlight. | Sunscreens and avoiding sun exposure can prevent melasma from becoming worse. Other treatment may include prescription creams to lighten the patches. | No treatment is necessary. Cosmetics can usually cover the blemish. | There is no cure for vitiligo. Treatment may include covering smaller patches with long-lasting dyes, light-sensitive drugs, in addition to ultraviolet A light therapy, corticosteroid creams, and depigmentation of the remaining skin. |
