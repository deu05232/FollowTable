# External Storage Options by Capacity Tier (Consumer-Facing)

| Capacity tier | Example external drive category | Typical single-drive capacities | Common connectors | Pros (capacity-focused) | Trade-offs |
| --- | --- | --- | --- | --- | --- |
| 250–500 GB | Portable external SSD | 250 GB / 500 GB | USB 3.2 Gen 2 (USB-C) | Small but fast for active projects | Higher cost per GB than HDD |
| 1 TB | Portable external HDD or SSD | 1 TB | USB 3.0 / USB 3.2 (USB-A or USB-C) | Good baseline for laptop backups | SSD: pricier; HDD: slower |
| 2 TB | Portable external HDD or SSD | 2 TB | USB 3.x | Popular balance of space and portability | Portable HDD can be shock-sensitive |
| 4 TB | High-capacity portable HDD or premium portable SSD | 4 TB | USB 3.x / USB-C | Fits multiple full-system images | SSD 4 TB often expensive |
| 5–6 TB | Max-capacity portable HDD | 5 TB / 6 TB | USB 3.x | Highest capacity in pocket-size HDDs | Bulkier; may draw more power |
| 8–12 TB | Desktop external HDD | 8 TB / 10 TB / 12 TB | USB 3.0 / USB-C | Big jump in capacity for backups | Needs AC power; not pocketable |
| 14–18 TB | Large desktop external HDD | 14 TB / 16 TB / 18 TB | USB 3.x | Best $/TB for very large archives | Large enclosure; slower random access |
| 20+ TB | Multi-bay external enclosure (RAID/JBOD) | 2×10 TB, 4×8 TB, 4×12 TB (varies) | USB-C / Thunderbolt | Scales capacity beyond single-drive limits | Higher upfront cost; setup complexity |
