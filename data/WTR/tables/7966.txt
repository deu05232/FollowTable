# Ibanez Spec-to-Feel Cheat Sheet  Common Choices and Their Typical On-Stage At-Home Effects

| Spec / design choice (feature) | Common Ibanez context (where you’ll see it) | Typical feel effect (impact) | Typical tone effect (impact) | Best suited for |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Thin “Wizard”-style neck | Many RG / metal-oriented models | Fast position shifts; easy stretches; can feel less supportive if you grip hard | Encourages precise, clean articulation because mistakes are less “hidden” by resistance | Shred/technical metal, fast lead work |
| More rounded modern neck carve (Oval C / similar) | AZ and some all-rounder lines | More hand support; less fatigue for chord-heavy playing | Helps consistent vibrato control; perceived as more even response string-to-string | Session work, blues-to-rock versatility |
| Flat vs more curved fretboard radius | Flatter on many modern Ibanez electrics; some models vary | Flatter = easier big bends without fretting out; curved = comfy open chords | Mostly indirect; flatter setups often pair with lower action for cleaner high-gain tracking | Lead-heavy styles (flat) vs chord-first styles (curvier) |
| Bolt-on neck joint | Common across RG/S/AZ | Snappy, immediate response; easier service/adjustments | Quicker attack; notes “pop” sooner, especially on clean funk-style playing | Modern rock, funk, tight rhythm |
| Set neck / semi-hollow construction | Artcore / AS-style | Softer attack feel; more resonance against the body | Warmer midrange bloom; earlier feedback at high gain | Jazz, indie, classic rock |
| High-output ceramic-leaning humbuckers | Metal-focused and some modern lines | Pick sensitivity feels more compressed; less effort to drive amp | Tight low end, aggressive mids; cleans can feel less airy | Modern metal, drop tunings |
| Lower/medium-output or alnico-voiced pickups | Many versatile or vintage-leaning options | More dynamic under the fingers; responds to pick strength | Wider clean headroom; sweeter top end; less compression | Blues, pop, classic rock, clean-to-crunch |
| Coil-split / switching systems | Frequently on versatile Ibanez configs | Lets you change “feel” via perceived compression and attack | Split tones add brighter, leaner sounds; not identical to true single-coils but useful | Cover bands, studio layering |
| Double-locking tremolo | Some RG/S variants | Requires different muting technique; flutter/dive effects possible | Slightly softer attack; can reduce sustain vs fixed depending on setup | Lead theatrics, whammy-heavy styles |
| Fixed bridge / hardtail | Many RGA/RGD/Iron Label | Palm-muting feels solid; simpler tuning stability | Stronger fundamental, more consistent sustain; tight rhythm tracking | Recording heavy rhythms, down-tuning |
| Longer scale length (baritone/extended) | RGD / 7–8 string oriented models | More tension; clearer intonation; less “rubbery” low strings | Better low-note definition; less low-end smear | Drop tunings, extended-range metal |
