  <caption>Earth flyby parameters from [ And2008 ] for spacecraft Galileo (GLL: flybys I and II), NEAR, Cassini, Rosetta and MESSENGER (M’GER). V ∞ is the average osculating hyperbolic asymptotic speed, α and δ are the right ascension and declination of the incoming (i) and outgoing (o) osculating asymptotic velocity vectors, and (O) Δ ⁢ V ∞ is the putative “excess speed” anomaly deduced by assuming that the speed of light is isotropic in modeling the doppler shifts, as in ( LABEL:eqn:olddoppler ). The observed (O) Δ ⁢ V ∞ values are from [ And2008 ] , and after correcting for atmospheric drag in the case of GLL-II, and thruster burn in the case of Cassini. (P) Δ ⁢ V ∞ is the predicted “excess speed”, using ( LABEL:eqn:anomaly ), taking account of the known light speed anisotropy and its effect upon the doppler shifts, using α v and δ v as the right ascension and declination of the 3-space flow velocity, having speed v , which has been taken to be 420km/s in all cases, except for NEAR, see Fig. LABEL:fig:CelestialPlot . The ± values on (P) Δ ⁢ V ∞ indicate changes caused by changing the declination by 5% - a sensitivity indicator. The angles θ i and θ f between the 3-space velocity and the asymptotic initial/final SV velocity V are also given. The observed doppler effect is in exceptional agreement with the predictions using ( LABEL:eqn:anomaly ) and the previously measured 3-space velocity. The flyby doppler shift is thus a new technique to accurately measure the dynamical 3-space velocity vector, albeit retrospectively from existing data. Note: By fine tuning the α v and δ v values for each flyby a perfect fit to the observed (O) Δ ⁢ V ∞ is possible. But here we have taken, for simplicity, the same values for GLL-I and NEAR.</caption>
